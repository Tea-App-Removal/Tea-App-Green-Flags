    New York Defamation and Privacy Laws

    New York has some of the most developed defamation law in the country, providing robust protections for those falsely accused in AWDTSG groups.

    Common law defamation in New York is well-established through extensive case law. A plaintiff must prove a false statement of fact, published to third parties, with fault (negligence for private figures), causing damages. New York courts have addressed internet defamation extensively, holding that Facebook posts constitute libel. Defamation per se in New York creates presumed damages for statements that charge a person with a serious crime, tend to injure professional reputation, impute a loathsome disease, or impute unchastity. These categories cover the vast majority of false AWDTSG accusations. CPLR § 215(3) establishes New York's one-year statute of limitations for defamation. This short window makes immediate action critical — both for pursuing removal and preserving litigation options. New York Civil Rights Law §§ 50-51 provide privacy protections against unauthorized use of a person's name, portrait, or picture for commercial purposes. While primarily aimed at commercial exploitation, these statutes add to the overall legal framework. New York Penal Law § 240.30 addresses aggravated harassment, which may apply to revenge-motivated AWDTSG posts intended to harass rather than genuinely warn.
