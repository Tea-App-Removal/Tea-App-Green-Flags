    Colorado Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Colorado's defamation framework provides meaningful protections for AWDTSG victims, with some unique features that strengthen removal efforts. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Colorado Revised Statutes § 13-21-102 governs defamation actions in the state. Colorado recognizes both libel and slander, and AWDTSG Facebook posts constitute libel. The state follows the common law requirement of a false statement of fact, publication, fault, and damages.

    Colorado recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute criminal conduct
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their business, trade, or profession
  • Impute sexual misconduct

    • Most AWDTSG posts fall into one or more of these categories, meaning damages are presumed without requiring proof of specific financial loss.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Colorado is one year under C.R.S. § 13-80-103. This shorter timeline makes rapid action critical for Colorado AWDTSG victims.

    Colorado's Criminal Libel statute (C.R.S. § 18-13-105) is notable — Colorado is one of the few states with an active criminal libel law. While prosecutions are rare, the existence of criminal liability for knowingly false and defamatory statements adds significant weight to removal demands.

    Colorado also provides protections under the Colorado Privacy Act (CPA), which took effect in 2023. This comprehensive privacy law gives Colorado residents rights over their personal data, including the right to deletion. AWDTSG posts sharing personal information, photos, and identifying details without consent may implicate CPA protections.

    C.R.S. § 18-9-111 addresses harassment, including electronic harassment, which can apply to targeted AWDTSG campaigns and coordinated posting.
