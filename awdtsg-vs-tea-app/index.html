AWDTSG: The Facebook Group Network

"Are We Dating the Same Guy" groups are city-specific Facebook communities restricted to women-only membership. They operate within Facebook's ecosystem, which means they're governed by Facebook's terms of service and moderation policies.

How AWDTSG works:
  • Women join their local city group after passing screening questions
  • Members post photos of men (usually from dating app profiles) with questions or warnings
  • Other members respond with their own experiences or information
  • Groups are closed — content is only visible to approved members
  • Membership ranges from 5,000 to 164,000+ depending on the city
    • Content characteristics:
  • Posts are tied to the poster's Facebook identity (though profiles can be minimal)
  • Comments create thread-style discussions that can grow to hundreds of responses
  • Group admins moderate with varying levels of attention
  • Content lives within Facebook's infrastructure
    • Spread pattern:
  • Screenshots get shared to Instagram Stories, private messages, Reddit, and Twitter
  • Content crosses between city-specific groups when members belong to multiple groups
  • Facebook's algorithm surfaces high-engagement posts to more group members
  • Original posts stay within the closed group, but copies spread everywhere

    Tea App: The Standalone Platform

    Tea App is an independent mobile application designed specifically for sharing anonymous reviews about people, with a heavy focus on dating experiences. Unlike AWDTSG, Tea App isn't part of any major social media platform.

    How Tea App works:
  • Users download the app and create an account
  • Anyone can search for a person by name and read existing reviews
  • Users post anonymous reviews about individuals they've encountered
  • Reviews include ratings, text descriptions, and sometimes photos
  • Content is publicly accessible — no group membership required
    • Content characteristics:
  • Reviews are anonymous, making poster identification difficult
  • Content is structured (ratings, categories) rather than free-form like AWDTSG
  • No comment threads — reviews stand alone
  • Content is indexed by search engines because it's publicly accessible
    • Spread pattern:
  • Google indexes Tea App content directly — reviews appear in search results for your name
  • Screenshots get shared to social media just like AWDTSG content
  • The app's public nature means anyone can view reviews without an account
  • Cross-referencing between Tea App and AWDTSG is common — someone posted in one often gets posted in the other

    • Key Differences That Affect Removal

    Understanding the structural differences between these platforms is critical for developing an effective removal strategy.

    Visibility and Access

    AWDTSG: Closed groups mean content is theoretically contained. In practice, screenshots leak constantly, but the original post is only visible to group members. This creates a strange dynamic where you can't see what's being said about you, but thousands of women can. Tea App: Fully public content that anyone can view. Your reviews are visible to potential dates, employers, friends, and family without any barrier. Google indexes this content, meaning it appears in search results for your name. In terms of raw searchability, Tea App is often more immediately damaging.

    Poster Accountability

    AWDTSG: Posters use their Facebook profiles, which provides at least some identifying information. In defamation cases, subpoenaing Facebook for poster identity is a well-established legal process. However, some posters use minimal profiles that reveal little. Tea App: Reviews are anonymous by design. Identifying the poster requires legal action against Tea App itself, requesting account information associated with the review. This process is more complex because the platform is designed around anonymity.

    Moderation Approach

    AWDTSG: Group admins handle moderation with wide variation in standards. Some admins remove clearly false posts; others maintain a strict "believe all women" policy that keeps posts up regardless of evidence. The quality of moderation depends entirely on who runs your local group. Tea App: The platform has its own content moderation policies and a reporting system. Response times and removal standards vary. Because Tea App is a dedicated company rather than a volunteer-run Facebook group, there are established channels for content disputes — though results are inconsistent.

    Legal Leverage

    AWDTSG: Defamation claims target the individual poster. Facebook's Section 230 protection means the platform itself isn't liable for user content. However, Facebook is required to respond to valid DMCA takedown notices for copyrighted images. Learn about your legal rights with AWDTSG posts. Tea App: Similar legal framework applies — the platform has Section 230 protection, but individual reviewers don't. The challenge is identifying anonymous reviewers through legal discovery. Tea App's terms of service and content policies provide additional avenues for removal requests.
