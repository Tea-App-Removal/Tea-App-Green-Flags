    North Dakota Defamation and Privacy Laws

    North Dakota provides detailed statutory defamation protections that create clear legal leverage for AWDTSG post removal.

    NDCC § 14-02-04 defines defamation as the publication of false statements that injure a person's reputation, exposing them to hatred, contempt, ridicule, or obloquy. This statutory definition directly applies to false AWDTSG Facebook group posts. All AWDTSG posts fall under Facebook's Community Standards, including their Bullying and Harassment Policy. NDCC § 14-02-05 specifically addresses libel — written defamation — which is precisely what Facebook group posts constitute. The statute defines libel as defamation expressed in print, writing, or other fixed medium, and establishes that damages may be presumed for certain categories of libelous statements. NDCC § 14-02-06 defines slander, while § 14-02-07 lists slander per se categories including imputation of crime, imputation of existing contagious or infectious disease, injury to trade or profession, and imputation of unchastity. While AWDTSG posts are technically libel, these per se categories inform the legal standard for presumed damages. NDCC § 14-02-08 addresses privileged communications, establishing what speech is and is not protected. False AWDTSG posts don't qualify for any recognized privilege under North Dakota law. Privacy protections in North Dakota include common law recognition of invasion of privacy torts. Public disclosure of private facts through AWDTSG posts creates additional legal avenues for removal and damages.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in North Dakota is two years (NDCC § 28-01-18). While reasonable, the small-state amplification effect means damage accumulates rapidly and early action is critical.
