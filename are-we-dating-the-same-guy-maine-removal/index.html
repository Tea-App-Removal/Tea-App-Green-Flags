    Maine Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Maine provides solid defamation protections through its statutory and common law framework. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Maine Revised Statutes Title 14, § 556 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at two years from the date of publication. Maine follows common law defamation principles recognizing both libel and slander.

    Maine recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute criminal conduct
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their business, trade, or profession
  • Impute unchastity or sexual misconduct

    • Under per se treatment, damages are presumed. Most AWDTSG posts qualify under these categories.

    Maine Revised Statutes Title 17-A, § 506-A addresses harassment, including electronic communications used to harass, torment, or threaten. Targeted AWDTSG campaigns may constitute criminal harassment under Maine law.

    Maine courts recognize invasion of privacy claims, including false light publicity and public disclosure of private facts. These torts provide additional legal tools when AWDTSG posts share private information or create misleading impressions.

    Maine's constitutional privacy protections under Article I, Section 5 of the Maine Constitution add further legal weight to removal demands involving unauthorized exposure of private information.
