    Ohio Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Ohio provides both statutory and common law defamation protections that create strong leverage for AWDTSG post removal.

    Ohio Revised Code § 2739.01 establishes statutory liability for libel, defining it as a false publication that tends to injure a person's reputation or expose them to public hatred, contempt, or ridicule. Facebook group posts clearly constitute libel under this statute. All AWDTSG posts fall under Facebook's Community Standards, including their Bullying and Harassment Policy. Common law defamation in Ohio is well-developed through extensive case law. Ohio courts require proof of a false statement of fact, publication to third parties, fault, and damages. Ohio has addressed internet defamation extensively, establishing clear precedent that social media posts are actionable. Defamation per se in Ohio provides presumed damages for statements that impute criminal conduct, a loathsome disease, injury to trade or profession, or serious sexual misconduct. These categories cover the vast majority of false AWDTSG accusations. ORC § 2315.21 governs punitive damages in Ohio, allowing awards where the defendant acted with actual malice. Revenge-motivated AWDTSG posts — a common origin for false content — frequently demonstrate the malice necessary for punitive damages. Privacy protections in Ohio include statutory and common law recognition of invasion of privacy torts. ORC § 2307.60 provides a civil remedy for criminal acts, potentially applicable when AWDTSG posts constitute harassment.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Ohio is one year (ORC § 2305.11). This short window requires immediate action to preserve both removal and litigation options.
