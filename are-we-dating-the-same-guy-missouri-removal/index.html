    Missouri Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Missouri provides strong defamation protections with a well-established legal framework that benefits AWDTSG victims. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Missouri Revised Statutes § 516.140 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at two years from publication. Missouri follows common law defamation principles recognizing both libel and slander, with AWDTSG posts constituting libel.

    Missouri recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute criminal conduct
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their trade, business, or profession
  • Impute unchastity or serious sexual misconduct

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed without proof of specific financial loss. Most AWDTSG posts qualify under these categories.

    Missouri Revised Statutes § 565.090 addresses harassment, including electronic harassment through social media platforms. Missouri's harassment statute is broad enough to cover targeted AWDTSG campaigns involving repeated posting or coordinated online attacks. Mo. Rev. Stat. § 565.225 through § 565.227 covers stalking, including cyberstalking through electronic communications. Persistent AWDTSG targeting of an individual may constitute stalking under Missouri law.

    Missouri's right of publicity statute (Mo. Rev. Stat. § 162.702) protects against unauthorized use of a person's name, voice, or likeness. AWDTSG posts sharing photos and identifying information without consent may implicate this protection.

    Missouri courts recognize invasion of privacy claims, including false light and public disclosure of private facts. These provide additional legal tools when AWDTSG posts share private information or create misleading impressions.
