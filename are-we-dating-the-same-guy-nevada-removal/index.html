    Nevada Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Nevada provides exceptionally strong legal protections against false AWDTSG posts — stronger than most states.

    NRS § 200.510 through § 200.560 establish Nevada's criminal defamation framework. Nevada is one of a minority of states that still criminalizes defamation, making it a gross misdemeanor to publish false and malicious statements that expose someone to hatred, contempt, ridicule, or obloquy. This criminal dimension adds significant leverage that doesn't exist in most other states. Civil defamation in Nevada follows the standard framework: a false statement of fact, published to third parties, with fault, causing damages. Nevada courts recognize defamation per se for statements imputing criminal conduct, professional incompetence, a loathsome disease, or serious sexual misconduct. AWDTSG posts frequently contain these types of accusations. NRS § 41.660 establishes Nevada's framework for damages in defamation cases, including compensatory, special, and punitive damages. Nevada courts have been willing to award substantial punitive damages in cases demonstrating actual malice — a standard frequently met by revenge-motivated AWDTSG posts from ex-partners. Nevada's privacy tort law recognizes claims for public disclosure of private facts and false light publicity. AWDTSG posts that share private personal information — medical details, financial status, intimate relationship details — create additional legal avenues beyond defamation.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Nevada is two years (NRS § 11.190). While this provides reasonable time to act, the digital damage from AWDTSG posts compounds daily. Acting quickly minimizes total harm.
