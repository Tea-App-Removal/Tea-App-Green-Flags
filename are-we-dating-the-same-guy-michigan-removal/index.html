    Michigan Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Michigan provides strong defamation protections with both civil and criminal dimensions. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Michigan Compiled Laws § 600.5805(9) sets the statute of limitations for defamation at one year from publication. This shorter timeline makes prompt action critical for Michigan AWDTSG victims.

    Michigan follows common law defamation principles, recognizing both libel and slander. AWDTSG posts constitute libel. Michigan requires proof of a false and defamatory statement, publication to a third party, fault, and damages.

    Michigan recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute a criminal offense
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their business, trade, or profession
  • Impute unchastity or serious sexual misconduct

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed. Most AWDTSG posts qualify.

    Michigan Compiled Laws § 750.370 addresses criminal defamation (libel). Michigan retains this criminal statute, which makes it a misdemeanor to maliciously publish defamatory material. While rarely prosecuted, it adds leverage to removal demands. MCL § 750.411h and § 750.411i cover stalking and cyberstalking in Michigan. These statutes apply to patterns of harassment through electronic communication, including targeted AWDTSG campaigns. MCL § 750.411s addresses the posting of sexually explicit images without consent, which applies if intimate content is shared in AWDTSG groups.

    Michigan also recognizes invasion of privacy claims, including false light and public disclosure of private facts.
