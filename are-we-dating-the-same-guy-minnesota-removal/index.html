    Minnesota Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Minnesota provides strong defamation protections with a well-developed legal framework. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Minnesota Statutes § 541.07(1) sets the statute of limitations for defamation at two years from publication. Minnesota follows common law defamation principles recognizing both libel and slander.

    Minnesota recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute criminal conduct
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their business, trade, or profession
  • Impute unchastity or serious sexual misconduct

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed. Most AWDTSG posts qualify because they typically contain false accusations of cheating, abuse, or criminal behavior. According to Pew Research Center, 41% of Americans have personally experienced some form of online harassment.

    Minnesota Statutes § 609.749 addresses stalking and harassment, including electronic communications. This comprehensive statute covers a range of harassing behaviors through social media platforms, including targeted AWDTSG campaigns. Minnesota Statutes § 604A.01 provides the Minnesota Data Practices and Privacy framework, which includes protections for personal information. While primarily focused on government data, the principles inform Minnesota courts' approach to privacy claims.

    Minnesota courts recognize invasion of privacy claims, including false light and public disclosure of private facts. These causes of action provide additional legal tools for AWDTSG removal when posts share private information or create misleading portrayals.

    Minnesota Statutes § 617.261 covers nonconsensual dissemination of intimate images, applicable if such content appears in AWDTSG posts.
