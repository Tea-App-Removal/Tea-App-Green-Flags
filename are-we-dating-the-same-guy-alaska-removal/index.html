    Alaska Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Alaska provides meaningful legal protections against the kind of false statements commonly found in AWDTSG posts.

    Alaska Statute § 09.65.131 governs defamation actions in the state. Under Alaska law, defamation requires proof of a false statement of fact published to third parties that causes harm to reputation. Alaska follows the general common law framework recognizing both libel (written/posted defamation) and slander (spoken defamation). Social media posts in AWDTSG groups constitute libel.

    Like many states, Alaska recognizes defamation per se — categories of false statements so inherently damaging that actual damages are presumed. These include false accusations of:

  • Criminal conduct
  • Sexual misconduct or unchastity
  • Conduct injurious to your profession or business
  • Having a serious communicable disease

    • AWDTSG posts frequently contain exactly these types of accusations, which strengthens removal leverage significantly under Alaska law.

    Alaska Statute § 09.10.070 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at two years from the date of publication. Alaska courts have recognized that online republication can extend this timeline.

    Alaska's right to privacy protections are also notable. The Alaska Constitution (Article I, Section 22) explicitly protects the right to privacy — one of the few state constitutions to do so. This constitutional privacy right provides additional legal leverage against AWDTSG posts that expose private information, intimate details, or personal photographs without consent.

    Additionally, Alaska Statute § 11.61.120 addresses online harassment, which can apply when AWDTSG posts cross the line from opinion into targeted harassment campaigns.
