    New Jersey Defamation and Privacy Laws

    New Jersey provides a strong legal framework for combating false AWDTSG posts.

    Common law defamation in New Jersey requires a false statement of fact, communicated to third parties, with fault, causing damages. New Jersey courts have consistently held that Facebook posts constitute libel — the written form of defamation — which carries stronger legal protections than spoken defamation. Defamation per se is well-established in New Jersey case law. Statements that accuse someone of criminal activity, impute a loathsome disease, affect professional reputation, or allege sexual misconduct create presumed damages without proof of specific financial harm. These categories cover the vast majority of false AWDTSG accusations. N.J.S.A. 2A:15-5.9 et seq. (Punitive Damages Act) allows punitive damages in defamation cases where the defendant acted with actual malice — knowingly publishing false statements or acting with reckless disregard for truth. Ex-partner revenge posts, fabricated accusations, and deliberately false AWDTSG content frequently meet this standard. New Jersey's privacy protections are among the strongest in the nation. The state recognizes all four privacy torts: intrusion upon seclusion, public disclosure of private facts, false light publicity, and appropriation. AWDTSG posts that share private personal details trigger these additional protections. N.J.S.A. 2C:33-4 (Harassment) provides criminal penalties for communications made with purpose to harass. Revenge-motivated AWDTSG posts may constitute criminal harassment in addition to civil defamation, creating dual legal leverage.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in New Jersey is one year (N.J.S.A. 2A:14-3). This tight window demands swift action to preserve legal options while pursuing professional removal.
