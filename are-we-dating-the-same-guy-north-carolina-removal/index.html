    North Carolina Defamation and Privacy Laws

    North Carolina provides strong statutory and common law protections against false AWDTSG posts.

    N.C.G.S. § 99A-1 establishes a clear statutory right to recover damages for libel and slander. This statute explicitly provides that anyone who publishes a libel is liable for damages. Facebook group posts constitute libel under North Carolina law as written, published statements. All AWDTSG posts fall under Facebook's Community Standards, including their Bullying and Harassment Policy. Defamation per se is well-established in North Carolina for statements that accuse someone of committing a crime, allege infection with a loathsome disease, injure someone in their trade or profession, or impute unchastity. These categories encompass the vast majority of false AWDTSG accusations, and presumed damages apply without proof of specific financial harm. N.C.G.S. § 99A-2 addresses retraction as a potential mitigation of damages but not a complete defense. This means that even if a poster attempts to retract or delete after being confronted, liability for damages already caused may still exist. Privacy protections in North Carolina include common law torts for public disclosure of private facts and false light publicity. AWDTSG posts sharing intimate personal details, medical information, or financial circumstances trigger these additional protections.

    North Carolina's statute of limitations for defamation is one year (N.C.G.S. § 1-54). This short window makes immediate action essential for preserving both removal options and litigation rights.
