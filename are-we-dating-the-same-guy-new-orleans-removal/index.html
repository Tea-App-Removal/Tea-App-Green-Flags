    Louisiana Defamation Laws and Your Rights

    Louisiana defamation law is rooted in the state's unique civil law tradition (derived from French law, not English common law). Under Louisiana Civil Code Article 2315, defamation is actionable as a fault-based tort. Louisiana recognizes defamation when false statements are communicated to third parties and cause damage to reputation.

    Louisiana courts have identified defamation per se categories including accusations of criminal conduct, allegations of moral turpitude, and statements that injure someone in their profession. False New Orleans AWDTSG posts commonly contain accusations fitting these categories, meaning damage is presumed without proof of specific financial loss.

    Louisiana's prescriptive period (statute of limitations) for defamation is one year from the date of publication. Given how rapidly New Orleans AWDTSG posts circulate through the city's interconnected social networks, acting within weeks — not months — produces the best outcomes.

    Louisiana's civil law tradition also provides unique remedies. Courts can award both compensatory and exemplary damages for intentional defamation, creating strong legal leverage for removal when false AWDTSG posts are motivated by revenge or malice.
