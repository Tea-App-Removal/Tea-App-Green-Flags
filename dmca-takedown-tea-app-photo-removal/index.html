The Legal Foundation: What the DMCA Actually Says

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act, signed into law in 1998, established a framework balancing the interests of copyright holders, internet platforms, and users. The key mechanism is under 17 U.S.C. Section 512: online platforms receive legal protection from copyright infringement liability, but only if they comply with the "notice and takedown" procedure. When a copyright holder sends a properly formatted notice claiming their copyrighted material is hosted without authorization, the platform must "expeditiously" remove or disable access to that material.

This is fundamentally different from requesting removal of defamatory text. When you report defamatory text to Tea App, Section 230 of the Communications Decency Act means the platform has no legal obligation to remove it. They can investigate, decide they don't see a violation, and decline to act. But when you file a DMCA takedown notice for your copyrighted photos, the calculus changes. If Tea App ignores a valid DMCA notice, they lose their legal immunity and can be sued for copyright infringement. That financial exposure motivates compliance in a way that defamation reports simply don't.

What Qualifies for DMCA Protection

Not every image in a Tea App post can be removed via DMCA. The key question is copyright ownership.

Photos you took yourself. Any photo you took with your own camera or phone is your copyrighted work. Selfies, mirror photos, timer shots, screenshots from video calls you recorded. You own the copyright automatically. These are the strongest candidates for DMCA takedowns. Photos taken by someone you hired. If you paid a photographer, the copyright situation depends on the contract. The photographer often retains copyright unless the contract explicitly transfers it. You may need their cooperation or a work-for-hire agreement. Photos taken by friends or family. If a friend took a photo of you, they're technically the copyright holder. You may need their authorization, though friends are usually willing to cooperate. Photos taken by the poster. If the person who posted used a photo they took themselves, such as a photo of you on a date, they hold the copyright. You cannot file a DMCA takedown for photos you don't own, even if you're the subject. This is a common and frustrating limitation. Screenshots of your dating profiles. Screenshots of your Hinge, Bumble, or Tinder profile that include photos you took still contain your copyrighted images. The screenshot format doesn't transfer copyright.

How to File a DMCA Takedown Against Tea App

There are two primary channels: directly to Tea App and through Apple's App Store.

Filing Directly With Tea App

Your DMCA notice must include these elements to be legally valid under 17 U.S.C. Section 512(c)(3):

A physical or electronic signature of the copyright owner. Identification of the copyrighted work, with the original file or a link to where you originally posted it. Identification of the infringing material and its specific location within Tea App. Your contact information: name, address, phone number, and email. A good faith statement that the use is not authorized. A statement under penalty of perjury that the information is accurate and you are the copyright owner.

The notice should be sent to Tea App's designated DMCA agent, whose contact information should be in the app's terms of service. If you cannot locate a designated agent, search the U.S. Copyright Office's DMCA Designated Agent Directory.

Filing Through Apple's App Store

Tea App is distributed through Apple's App Store, and Apple has its own DMCA process. Apple takes copyright infringement seriously because their Developer Program Agreement requires apps to respect intellectual property rights. Submit a DMCA notice through Apple's copyright infringement reporting page, identifying the app and where within it the infringing content appears. Apple's response can include removing content, notifying Tea App, or threatening removal of the app from the Store.

Filing through Apple is particularly effective when direct filings go unanswered. The threat of App Store consequences creates additional pressure.

What DMCA Can and Cannot Remove

Can remove: Your copyrighted photographs and images, including photos that have been cropped, filtered, or otherwise modified. Minor modifications don't eliminate your copyright claim. Cannot remove: The text of the post. The words written by the poster are their own expression, not your copyrighted work. A post saying "John from Dallas gave me chlamydia" with your photo will, after a successful DMCA takedown, still contain the text, just without the photo. Cannot remove: Photos you don't own. Claiming copyright over photos taken by someone else is perjury under the DMCA. Cannot remove: Photos that constitute fair use, though this defense rarely succeeds in the context of Tea App defamation posts.

The practical implication is that DMCA is powerful but incomplete. Removing your photo eliminates visual identification and reduces the post's viral potential. But for complete removal of both photos and text, DMCA needs to be combined with defamation-based removal through platform legal channels.

Timelines: How Long DMCA Takedowns Take

Tea App direct filing: 5 to 15 business days typical response time. Smaller platforms often have less developed legal infrastructure, which can mean slower processing. Apple App Store filing: 3 to 10 business days. Apple has a well-staffed intellectual property team and processes notices more efficiently. Counter-notice complication: Under the DMCA, the poster can file a counter-notice asserting lawful use. If filed, the platform must restore content within 10 to 14 business days unless you file a federal court action. Counter-notices are relatively uncommon in Tea App cases. Professional filing: 3 to 7 business days in many cases. Professional removal services that handle DMCA filings regularly know exactly what each platform requires and how to format notices for fastest processing.

Common Mistakes That Delay DMCA Takedowns

Incomplete notices. Missing any required statutory element gives the platform grounds to reject your notice. Platforms are not required to follow up and request missing information. Vague identification. "My photo is on Tea App" doesn't tell the platform which post to address. You need the specific post URL, in-app location, or screenshot showing exactly where the material appears. Filing for photos you don't own. This is perjury under the DMCA. Beyond the legal risk, platforms that detect invalid claims may flag your future filings. Sending to the wrong address. DMCA notices must go to the platform's designated agent. A general support email or press contact may never reach the right department. Not following up. If you haven't received acknowledgment within 5 business days, follow up. Notices sometimes fall through the cracks at smaller platforms.
