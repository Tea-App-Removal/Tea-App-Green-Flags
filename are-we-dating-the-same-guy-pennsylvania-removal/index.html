    Pennsylvania Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Pennsylvania provides one of the strongest legal frameworks in the country for combating false AWDTSG posts.

    42 Pa.C.S. § 8343 establishes the burden of proof in defamation cases, requiring the plaintiff to prove the defamatory character of the communication, its publication to third parties, its application to the plaintiff, understanding of its defamatory meaning, and resulting special harm (unless per se categories apply). 18 Pa.C.S. § 4504 establishes criminal defamation in Pennsylvania, making it a misdemeanor to maliciously publish or make defamatory statements. Pennsylvania is one of a minority of states maintaining criminal defamation statutes, providing exceptional leverage for AWDTSG post removal. Defamation per se is well-established in Pennsylvania for statements imputing criminal offense, loathsome disease, conduct or characteristics incompatible with business or profession, or serious sexual misconduct. These categories cover virtually all false AWDTSG accusations and trigger presumed damages. 42 Pa.C.S. § 8344 addresses the effect of retraction on damages but does not eliminate liability for defamation already published and shared.

    Pennsylvania courts recognize invasion of privacy torts including public disclosure of private facts, false light publicity, and intrusion upon seclusion. AWDTSG posts that combine false accusations with private personal information trigger multiple legal avenues.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Pennsylvania is one year (42 Pa.C.S. § 5523). Combined with the massive size of Philadelphia and Pittsburgh groups, immediate action is critical.
