    California Defamation and Privacy Laws

    California provides exceptional legal protections against defamation and privacy violations, making it one of the best states for pursuing AWDTSG post removal.

    California Civil Code § 44 through § 48 governs defamation in California. The state recognizes libel (Cal. Civ. Code § 45) as a false and unprivileged publication that exposes someone to hatred, contempt, or ridicule, or injures their reputation. AWDTSG Facebook posts clearly fall within this definition. California Civil Code § 45a defines libel per se — statements that are defamatory on their face without the need for extrinsic evidence. Most AWDTSG posts accusing men of cheating, abuse, criminal behavior, or disease qualify as libel per se, meaning damages are presumed.

    California's statute of limitations for defamation is one year under California Code of Civil Procedure § 340(c). This shorter timeline makes prompt action essential. However, the single publication rule has been modified by California courts to recognize that each new posting or sharing can constitute a new publication.

    What makes California truly powerful for AWDTSG removal is the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and the California Privacy Rights Act (CPRA). These landmark privacy laws give California residents the right to request deletion of personal information collected and shared without consent. AWDTSG posts that share names, photos, personal details, and location information may trigger CCPA/CPRA protections.

    California's Right of Publicity (Cal. Civ. Code § 3344) protects against the unauthorized use of your name and likeness, adding yet another legal tool for removal. California Penal Code § 653.2 — California's cyber-exploitation and harassment statute — applies when AWDTSG posts are used as weapons for harassment or intimidation. California also has robust anti-revenge porn laws (Cal. Civ. Code § 1708.85) that apply if intimate images are shared in AWDTSG groups.

    California's Anti-SLAPP statute (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16) is worth noting. While it protects speech on matters of public concern, California courts have repeatedly held that false statements of fact about private individuals do not qualify for Anti-SLAPP protection, particularly in defamation per se cases.
