The Three Categories of AWDTSG Removal

All AWDTSG removal approaches fall into three categories:

  • DIY methods — free, self-directed, low success rate

    Category 1: DIY Removal Methods

    Facebook Community Standards Report

    Cost: $0 Success Rate: 5-10% Timeline: 1-7 days for review (if it works)

    You can report an AWDTSG post directly to Facebook for violating community standards. The most common categories used:

  • Harassment or bullying
  • Sharing personal information without consent
  • False information
  • Privacy violations
    • Why it usually fails: Facebook's automated systems process millions of reports daily. Posts that don't contain explicit threats, hate speech, or graphic content are rarely flagged for removal. AWDTSG posts — which typically consist of a photo and text about someone — don't trigger Facebook's automated removal thresholds. Manual review, when it happens, often determines the content is "opinion" or "personal experience sharing" rather than a policy violation. When it works: Posts that contain explicit threats, real phone numbers or addresses (doxxing), graphic sexual content, or clear violations of Facebook's policies are more likely to be removed through standard reporting.

    DIY DMCA Takedown

    Cost: $0 Success Rate: ~10% Timeline: 3-14 days for processing

    If the AWDTSG post contains a photo you took of yourself (selfie, self-timer shot), you can file a DMCA copyright takedown with Facebook. Read our complete DMCA takedown guide for the step-by-step process.

    Why it usually fails: Most filers don't actually own the copyright to the image used, Facebook's DMCA form is unforgiving of errors, and posters can file counter-notices that restore the content. Even when successful, DMCA only removes the image — not the defamatory text. When it works: Clear-cut cases where you definitively own the photo copyright, the poster doesn't file a counter-notice, and the post hasn't spread to other platforms.

    Contacting the Poster Directly

    Cost: $0 Success Rate: <5% Timeline: Unpredictable

    Some guides suggest messaging the person who posted about you and requesting removal.

    Why it almost always fails — and often backfires: In most cases, contacting the poster results in the message itself becoming a new AWDTSG post ("Look, he found my post and is trying to shut me up"), generating a new wave of attention and engagement. The poster has no incentive to remove content and significant social incentive to publicize your request. When it works: Extremely rare cases where the poster made a genuine mistake (mistaken identity) and is reasonable enough to correct it. Don't count on this.

    DIY Summary

    | Facebook Report | $0 | 5-10% | 1-7 days | Yes (if it works) |

    Bottom line: DIY methods cost nothing, so they're worth attempting as a first step. But with combined success rates under 15%, most people who rely solely on DIY approaches end up with the post still active and spreading while they've lost valuable time.

    Category 2: Legal Approaches

    Cease and Desist Letter

    Cost: $500-$2,000 (attorney fees) Success Rate: 30-50% Timeline: 2-8 weeks

    An attorney drafts a formal letter to the poster demanding removal of the defamatory content, citing specific legal claims (defamation, invasion of privacy, intentional infliction of emotional distress) and threatening litigation if the content isn't removed. If you're struggling, resources like the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (call or text 988) provide free, confidential support.     Advantages:
  • Demonstrates legal seriousness
  • Creates a paper trail for potential future litigation
  • Some posters remove content when confronted with legal consequences
  • Can include demands beyond just removal (destruction of screenshots, agreement not to repost)
    • Disadvantages:
  • Requires identifying the poster (not always easy with Facebook privacy settings)
  • Letter has no legal enforcement power — it's a threat, not a court order
  • Some posters respond by posting the letter itself to the AWDTSG group, creating new viral content
  • Does not address copies that have spread to other platforms
  • Attorney fees apply regardless of outcome

    • Defamation Lawsuit

    Cost: $15,000-$150,000+ (through trial) Success Rate: 60-80% (of cases that proceed) Timeline: 6-24 months (often longer)

    Filing a civil lawsuit for defamation, invasion of privacy, or related claims against the poster. Learn more about your legal rights in AWDTSG defamation cases.

    Advantages:
  • Court orders carry legal enforcement power
  • Can result in financial damages (compensatory and punitive)
  • Creates legal precedent
  • Discovery process can identify anonymous posters
  • Injunctions can prevent future posting
    • Disadvantages:
  • Extremely expensive — retainers start at $5,000-$15,000 and total costs frequently exceed $50,000
  • Very slow — cases take months to years
  • The lawsuit itself can become public and generate media attention (see major AWDTSG lawsuits)
  • Must prove the statements are provably false (not just unfair opinions)
  • Anti-SLAPP laws in many states allow defendants to seek dismissal and recover attorney fees
  • Winning doesn't guarantee the poster will pay or comply
  • Emotional toll is significant

    • Hiring an Internet Defamation Attorney (Consultation Only)

    Cost: $200-$500 (initial consultation) Success Rate: N/A (advisory only) Timeline: 1-2 weeks for consultation

    Many people consult with an attorney to understand their options without committing to a full legal engagement. This is often a smart first step, especially for severe cases.

    What you get: Professional assessment of the content's legal vulnerability, advice on which claims are viable, recommendations for next steps (including whether professional removal services might be more cost-effective than litigation).

    How to Choose the Right Removal Option

    Your best option depends on several factors:

    Choose DIY If:

  • The post is very recent (within 24 hours) and hasn't spread
  • You clearly own the copyright to the photo used
  • The post is relatively mild and low-engagement
  • You have time and willingness to file multiple reports
  • Budget is an absolute constraint

  • The content is severely defamatory with provably false factual claims

    What to Watch Out For

    The AWDTSG removal space, like any industry built around people in distress, has its share of questionable operators. Red flags to watch for:

    Guaranteeing 100% removal. No legitimate service can guarantee 100% success because removal depends on third-party platform decisions. Services claiming guaranteed removal are either lying or don't understand their own business. Requiring large upfront payments with no milestones. Reputable services structure payments around milestones or results. A demand for $5,000 upfront with vague delivery promises is concerning. Vague methodology. If a service can't explain how they achieve removals (in general terms — they don't need to reveal proprietary processes), they may not have a real methodology. No documented track record. Ask for case studies, success rate data, and references. Services that can't provide any evidence of past success may be new or ineffective. Pressure sales tactics. Yes, time matters in AWDTSG removal. But a service that uses extreme pressure ("your life will be ruined if you don't sign up in the next hour") is manipulating your distress, not serving your interests. Long-term contract requirements. AWDTSG post removal is a project with a defined scope and timeline. Monthly retainers or year-long contracts for a single removal case are a red flag.
