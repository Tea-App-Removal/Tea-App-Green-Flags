    Arkansas Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Arkansas provides solid legal protections against false statements in AWDTSG groups, with both statutory and common law frameworks supporting removal.

    Arkansas Code § 16-63-201 through § 16-63-207 governs defamation in the state. Arkansas recognizes both libel (which covers AWDTSG Facebook posts) and slander. To succeed in a defamation claim, you must show a false statement of fact, publication to third parties, fault, and resulting damages.

    Arkansas recognizes defamation per se for specific categories of false statements, including:

  • Accusations of criminal conduct
  • Claims of sexual misconduct or unchastity
  • Statements harming your trade, business, or profession
  • Accusations of having a loathsome disease

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed. Since AWDTSG posts routinely accuse men of cheating, abuse, predatory behavior, or criminal conduct, most Arkansas AWDTSG posts qualify for this heightened treatment.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Arkansas is three years under Arkansas Code § 16-56-104, which is longer than many states. This gives victims more time to pursue legal remedies, though acting quickly is always better to minimize ongoing damage.

    Arkansas Code § 5-71-215 addresses cyberbullying, which can apply to coordinated AWDTSG harassment campaigns. While primarily aimed at protecting minors, the statute reflects Arkansas's legal recognition of online harassment as a serious issue — a widespread problem that Pew Research Center found affects 41% of Americans.

    Arkansas courts have also recognized false light invasion of privacy as a cause of action, providing additional legal leverage when AWDTSG posts portray you in a misleading or harmful way even beyond traditional defamation claims.
