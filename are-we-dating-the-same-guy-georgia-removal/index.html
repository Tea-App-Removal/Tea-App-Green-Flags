    Georgia Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Georgia provides comprehensive legal protections against defamation with both statutory and constitutional provisions supporting removal efforts. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    O.C.G.A. § 51-5-1 through § 51-5-11 governs defamation in Georgia. The state defines libel under § 51-5-1 as a false and malicious defamation of another, expressed in print, writing, pictures, or signs, tending to injure the reputation of the person and exposing them to public hatred, contempt, or ridicule. AWDTSG Facebook posts clearly meet this definition.

    Georgia recognizes defamation per se under O.C.G.A. § 51-5-4 for statements that:

  • Charge a crime punishable by law
  • Charge someone with having an infectious disease
  • Injure someone in their trade, office, or profession
  • Impute unchastity to a person

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed without requiring specific proof of financial loss.

    The statute of limitations for defamation in Georgia is one year under O.C.G.A. § 9-3-33. This is among the shortest in the country, making immediate action critical for Georgia AWDTSG victims.

    Georgia's right to privacy is also constitutionally protected. The Georgia Constitution (Article I, Section I, Paragraph I) recognizes the right to privacy, and Georgia courts have developed robust invasion of privacy case law. O.C.G.A. § 51-5-11 specifically preserves common law privacy actions alongside statutory defamation claims. O.C.G.A. § 16-11-39.1 addresses cyberstalking in Georgia, applying to electronic communications used to harass, intimidate, or threaten. Targeted AWDTSG campaigns may trigger this criminal statute. Georgia's Anti-SLAPP statute (O.C.G.A. § 9-11-11.1) protects speech on matters of public concern, but false statements of fact about private individuals posted in AWDTSG groups generally fall outside Anti-SLAPP protection.
