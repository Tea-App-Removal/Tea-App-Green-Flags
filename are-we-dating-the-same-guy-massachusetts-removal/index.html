    Massachusetts Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Massachusetts provides robust defamation protections through a well-developed common law framework, supplemented by statutory provisions. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 260, § 4 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at three years from publication — one of the longest in the country. This extended timeline provides significant legal leverage for removal efforts.

    Massachusetts follows common law defamation principles and recognizes both libel and slander. AWDTSG Facebook posts constitute libel. The state requires proof of a false statement of fact, publication, fault, and damages.

    Massachusetts recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute criminal conduct
  • Impute a loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their business, trade, or profession
  • Impute unchastity or serious sexual misconduct

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed.

    Massachusetts General Laws Chapter 214, § 1B explicitly provides a right to privacy — a statutory right that gives individuals the ability to sue for unreasonable, substantial, or serious interference with their privacy. This is one of the strongest statutory privacy protections in the country and provides powerful additional leverage for AWDTSG removal. MGL Chapter 265, § 43A covers criminal harassment, including electronic harassment. Massachusetts's harassment law is comprehensive and applicable to targeted AWDTSG campaigns. MGL Chapter 258E provides harassment prevention orders that can be obtained through civil court without a criminal charge.
