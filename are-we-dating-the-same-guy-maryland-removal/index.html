    Maryland Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Maryland provides strong defamation protections with a well-developed body of case law. The FTC's Bureau of Consumer Protection provides additional resources for consumers facing online fraud and privacy violations.

    Maryland Courts and Judicial Proceedings Code § 5-105 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at one year from publication. This shorter timeline makes immediate action critical.

    Maryland follows common law defamation principles, recognizing both libel and slander. AWDTSG Facebook posts constitute libel. Maryland requires proof of a false statement of fact, publication, fault, and damages (unless per se categories apply).

    Maryland recognizes defamation per se for false statements that:

  • Impute a criminal offense
  • Impute an infectious or loathsome disease
  • Injure someone in their trade, business, or profession
  • Impute unchastity

    • Under per se treatment, general damages are presumed.

    Maryland Criminal Law Code § 3-803 addresses harassment, including electronic harassment. Coordinated AWDTSG campaigns may constitute criminal harassment under Maryland law. Maryland Criminal Law Code § 3-805 covers misuse of electronic communications to harass, alarm, or threaten. AWDTSG posts designed to harm rather than warn may trigger this statute.

    Maryland's common law privacy torts are well-established, including false light invasion of privacy and public disclosure of private facts. These provide additional legal tools for AWDTSG removal when posts share private information or create misleading portrayals.
