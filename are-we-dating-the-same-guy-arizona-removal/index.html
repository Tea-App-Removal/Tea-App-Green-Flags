    Arizona Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Arizona provides strong statutory and common law protections against the defamation that plagues AWDTSG groups.

    Arizona Revised Statutes § 12-651 through § 12-654 govern defamation actions in Arizona. The state recognizes both libel and slander and follows the general common law framework requiring proof of a false statement of fact, publication to third parties, fault, and damages. AWDTSG Facebook posts constitute libel under Arizona law.

    Arizona recognizes defamation per se for certain categories of false statements, including:

  • False accusations of criminal conduct
  • False claims of sexual misconduct
  • Statements injurious to your profession or business
  • False claims of having a loathsome disease

    • Under the per se doctrine, damages are presumed — you don't need to prove specific financial loss. Since AWDTSG posts commonly accuse men of cheating, abuse, harassment, or criminal behavior, most posts qualify for per se treatment under Arizona law.

    Arizona Revised Statutes § 12-541 sets the statute of limitations for defamation at one year from publication. This is shorter than many states, which makes prompt action especially important in Arizona. However, the discovery rule may extend this period if you weren't immediately aware of the post, and republication can restart the clock.

    Arizona courts have also recognized false light invasion of privacy claims, which can apply when AWDTSG posts portray you in a misleading way even if individual statements aren't technically provable as false. This provides additional legal leverage for removal.

    Arizona's Anti-SLAPP statute (A.R.S. § 12-752) is worth noting — it protects free speech on matters of public concern. However, false statements of fact about private individuals in AWDTSG groups generally do not qualify for Anti-SLAPP protection, particularly when they constitute defamation per se.
