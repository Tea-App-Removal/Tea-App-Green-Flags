    New Hampshire Defamation and Privacy Laws

    New Hampshire offers strong legal protections that create real leverage for AWDTSG post removal.

    RSA § 644:11 — Criminal Defamation. New Hampshire is one of a small number of states maintaining a criminal defamation statute. Knowingly communicating false information that exposes someone to public hatred, contempt, or ridicule is a misdemeanor. This criminal dimension significantly strengthens removal demands. Civil defamation in New Hampshire follows established common law principles. A plaintiff must prove a false statement of fact, publication to third parties, fault (negligence for private figures), and damages. New Hampshire courts have consistently held that internet publications — including Facebook group posts — satisfy the publication requirement. Defamation per se is recognized in New Hampshire for statements imputing criminal conduct, professional incompetence, a loathsome disease, or unchastity. AWDTSG posts making these types of false accusations trigger presumed damages without proof of specific financial harm. Research from Pew shows 41% of Americans have experienced online harassment, with lasting reputational harm. RSA § 507:8-d establishes New Hampshire's framework for limiting frivolous defamation claims while preserving genuine ones. This statute helps distinguish legitimate defamation cases — like false AWDTSG posts — from attempts to silence protected speech.

    New Hampshire courts recognize invasion of privacy torts including public disclosure of private facts. AWDTSG posts frequently combine defamation with private information sharing, creating multiple legal avenues for removal.

    The statute of limitations is three years for defamation in New Hampshire (RSA § 508:4), one of the more generous timelines in the country. However, digital reputation damage compounds daily, making early action essential.
