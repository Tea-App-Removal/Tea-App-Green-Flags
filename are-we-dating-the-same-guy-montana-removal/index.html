    Montana Defamation and Privacy Laws

    Montana provides strong legal protections for individuals falsely accused in AWDTSG groups. Understanding these laws is critical to leveraging them for post removal.

    Montana Code Annotated § 27-1-803 defines defamation as a false and unprivileged publication that exposes a person to hatred, contempt, ridicule, or obloquy, or causes them to be shunned or avoided, or has a tendency to injure them in their occupation. Montana is notable for treating libel and slander under a unified defamation framework rather than maintaining separate causes of action. MCA § 27-1-804 addresses defamation per se — statements so inherently damaging that actual damages are presumed without proof. Accusations of criminal behavior, sexual misconduct, or professional incompetence posted in AWDTSG groups frequently qualify as defamation per se under Montana law.

    Montana also recognizes invasion of privacy torts under common law, including public disclosure of private facts and false light publicity. Many AWDTSG posts combine defamatory statements with private personal information, creating multiple legal avenues for removal and damages.

    Punitive damages are available in Montana defamation cases where actual malice is demonstrated (MCA § 27-1-221). Revenge-motivated AWDTSG posts — made by an ex-partner seeking to harm rather than genuinely warn — often meet this standard.

    Montana's two-year statute of limitations for defamation (MCA § 27-2-204) means you have time to act, but the digital damage compounds daily. The sooner you pursue removal, the less damage accumulates.
